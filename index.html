Tsunamis

How it happens

A tsunami occurs when there is a sudden and massive disturbance underwater, such as an undersea earthquake, volcanic eruption, landslide, or even a meteor impact. These events displace a large volume of water, causing powerful waves to spread across the ocean at high speeds. As these waves approach the shore, they build up in height and energy, resulting in the potentially devastating impact when they hit coastal areas.

Famous examples

Indian Ocean Tsunami (2004): Triggered by a 9.1 magnitude earthquake off Sumatra, causing over 230,000 deaths across several countries.


Tohoku Tsunami (2011): Caused by a 9.0 magnitude earthquake in Japan, leading to over 18,000 deaths and the Fukushima nuclear disaster.


Lisbon Tsunami (1755): Followed a massive 8.5 magnitude earthquake, killing 60,000-100,000 people in Portugal.


Krakatoa Tsunami (1883): Resulted from the Krakatoa volcano eruption in Indonesia, causing over 36,000 deaths.

Impacts

Tsunamis can cause catastrophic impacts, including large-scale loss of life and injuries, extensive damage to property and infrastructure, and severe economic consequences. They also disrupt communities, displace people, and create public health concerns. The environmental effects are significant, as tsunamis can destroy coastal ecosystems and pollute habitats. Survivors often experience long-lasting psychological trauma. The combined effect of these impacts makes tsunamis one of the most devastating natural disasters.
